Noah Singer

I study computer science and math at Harvard (Pforzheimer House '22). I am fascinated by trying to understand what problems can or cannot be solved by computational processes, how such problems can best be solved, and what effect solving such problems has on society. I have broad interests in theoretical computer science, as well as in systems and machine learning. I hope to pursue a career in research after graduating. I also have years of experience with software and web engineering.

I use this site to collect some of my current and past papers, talks, projects, and my current and past coursework. I occasionally write on my blog.

In Spring 2020, some of my current projects included writing a survey on the Fiat-Shamir heuristic in cryptography and investigating generalization in deep neural networks via pithy experiments. In Summer 2020, I am supported by the Herchel-Smith Fellowship at Harvard, studying the log-rank conjecture in communication complexity with Prof. Madhu Sudan.


Activities

Work Experience: In Summer 2019, I worked as a software engineering intern at Airbnb in San Francisco on the Host Growth & Traffic team, where I built a production data pipeline to discover and manage large quantities of Google search advertising keywords targeting Airbnb hosts. I was responsible for efficiently scaling up new listing creation due to search ads and generating tens of thousands of dollars in weekly revenue.

Teaching: I was a Teaching Fellow for CS 161: Operating Systems in Spring 2020. I was a Teaching Fellow for CS 121: Introduction to Theoretical Computer Science in Fall 2019, organizing guest lectures in the "CS 121.5" recitation section for advanced students. I will be a Teaching Fellow for CS 121 again in Fall 2020. I have also worked as a peer tutor with the Academic Resource Center in computer science and math classes.

Volunteering: I am involved in programs that teach computer science and programming. In Spring 2019, I worked with Harvard's Digital Literacy Project to teach Scratch and Processing.js to Boston-area middle school students. I also taught a seminar entitled "Integer multiplication, Karatsuba's algorithm, and complexity bounds in theoretical computer science" at Harvard Splash 2019 that introduces students to the building blocks of theoretical computer science.

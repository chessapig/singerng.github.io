Noah Singer

Hey there! My name is Noah Singer, and I'm an incoming freshman computer science concentrator at Harvard interested in basically everything: the theory of computation, signal processing, machine learning, artificial intelligence, numerical optimization, algorithms and data structures, systems, compilers, programming languages, and cryptography. I also do a lot of software and web engineering in my spare time and during summers. I previously attended the Science, Math, and Computer Science Magnet Program at Montgomery Blair High School in Silver Spring, Maryland, and was really involved in the CS community there; I helped run the Computer Team and Cybersecurity Team by organizing and delivering weekly lectures.

On this site, you can see some of the lectures and talks I've given over the years and many of my major projects. Scroll down to learn more!


Activities

Research: I worked with Associate Professor Tom Goldstein at the University of Maryland, College Park in Summer 2017 to implement a Python version of his algorithm FASTA for efficient, user-friendly first-order optimization of (possibly) nonsmooth convex objectives using forward-backward splitting, the Barzilai-Borwein adaptive heuristic, and FISTA-style acceleration. In Summer 2016, I worked with Professor Clay Shields at Georgetown University, where I helped analyze and implement protocols to slow down malicious database exfiltration in postgreSQL databases.

Projects: I've worked on many software projects for fun, education, and service to my school over the years, including Darkside OS, a full-fledged, from-scratch operating system including a kernel, bootloader, and drivers, with more than 30,000 lines of code (2012-present), Kraken, an in-progress toy compiler with homegrown implementations of lexing and parsing algorithms, and SilverChips Online, a new site for my high school's newspaper. For more details, see here.

Leadership: I headed the Computer Team at my high school and gave weekly lectures on subjects as diverse as complexity theory, CPU architecture, Fourier analysis, and neural networks. I also directed ångstromCTF, a free, online, worldwide cybersecurity competition for high school students that drew more than 1500 teams in 2018.
